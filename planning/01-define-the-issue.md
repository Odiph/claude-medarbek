# Goal 1 — Define the Issue

**Base of the issue: what people hate about group collaboration.**

We don't start from a customer and hunt for pain. We start from a pain we can *feel* — the friction of getting a group of people to coordinate, decide, and follow through — and then find where it bites hardest. HOA is a candidate *context*, not the starting point.

A good issue is: **a real, recurring collaboration friction** · **specific about who feels it most** · **not a solution in disguise** · **big enough to own for years.**

---

## Step 0 — What do we hate about group collaboration?
Start with your own experience. Brainstorm the friction, raw and specific — real moments, not abstractions.

- Where does group coordination always break? (deciding, chasing people, losing track, no follow-through, unclear who owns what)
- What makes you dread organizing or participating in a group thing?
- The last time a group effort frustrated you — what exactly went wrong?

> Dump 10–15 of these. Specific incidents beat categories.

## Step 1 — Cluster into recurring frictions
Group the dump into a handful of recurring frictions. Name each in plain language.

> Candidate frictions: ____________________

## Step 2 — Pick the sharpest friction + who feels it most
One friction, one group of people who feel it acutely and repeatedly. *This* is where the issue lives.

> Friction: **[the collaboration pain]**
> Felt most by: **[role / group]** in the context of **[situation]** — e.g., HOA board members running a 200-home community.

## Step 3 — Cost of the status quo
No cost, no issue. Cost = time, money, missed outcomes, or the thing just not happening.

> Today this costs them: ____________________

## Step 4 — Write the issue statement
Fill one. Only one.

> **[Group]** repeatedly struggles to **[collaborate / coordinate on a job]**, because **[friction]**. Today they cope with **[workaround: group chats, spreadsheets, nagging]**, which costs them **[time/money/the outcome]**.

**Solution-in-disguise check:** if the sentence names "an app that…" or "a widget that…", delete it and re-describe the *friction*. The issue must be true even if we never build anything.

---

## Step 5 — Pressure-test the issue (all four must pass)

| Test | Question | Pass? |
|------|----------|:---:|
| **Real** | Can we point to someone (including us) who felt this friction this month? | |
| **Ours** | Is group collaboration a space we can credibly win and want to live in for years? | |
| **Recurring** | Does the friction hit often enough to build a business on? | |
| **Big enough** | Do many groups share this same friction, beyond one context? | |

If any fails, fix the issue before moving on. **A weak issue can't be rescued by a clever use case.**

---

## Why start from group collaboration
- **We're a data source.** We've all hated group coordination — that's real signal we can mine on day one, before any interview.
- **It's broad and durable.** The issue outlives any single context; HOA can be the first place we prove it, not the whole company.
- **It travels.** If the friction is real, it shows up in HOAs, teams, clubs, events — which is what "big enough" needs.

**Output of Goal 1:** one collaboration-friction issue statement that passes all four tests, with the context where it bites hardest. Carry it into [Goal 2](./02-decide-the-use-case.md).
